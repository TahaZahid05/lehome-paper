\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

Our methodology involves an iterative exploration of models and representations, progressing from standard diffusion-based policies to large-scale Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models, and finally to optimized action chunking architectures. We specifically focus on the challenges of bimanual coordination and the impact of visual backbones on task performance.

\subsection{Phase 1: Diffusion Policy Baseline}
We began our experiments with Diffusion Policy (DP) \cite{chi2023diffusionpolicy}, which models action trajectories as a denoising process. While effective for diverse manipulation tasks, DP struggled with the high-precision coordination required for bimanual garment folding. We observed that the model often produced inconsistent joint targets for the two arms, leading to catastrophic entanglement of the fabric.

\subsection{Phase 2: Vision-Language-Action Models}
To leverage semantic understanding, we explored Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models. We first evaluated SmolVLA \cite{smolvla2025}, a compact model integrating visual and language tokens. We then progressed to $\pi_{0.5}$ \cite{pi05_2026}, which utilizes continuous flow matching, and X-VLA \cite{zheng2025xvlasoftpromptedtransformerscalable}, which employs soft-prompting for cross-modal alignment. While these models showed improved robustness in identifying garment parts, their performance was limited by the scale of our specialized demonstration dataset. The large number of parameters in these models made them prone to overfitting on the limited data available for the LeHome Challenge.

\subsection{Phase 3: Action Chunking with Transformers (ACT)}
Our final architecture was Action Chunking with Transformers (ACT) \cite{zhao2023act}. ACT addresses the problem of compounding errors by predicting chunks of actions simultaneously using a Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) and a Transformer decoder. This approach is particularly well-suited for bimanual tasks as it captures the temporal correlations between the two arms. With only 80 million parameters, ACT proved to be more data-efficient and easier to train for the specific dynamics of garment manipulation.

\subsection{Geometric and Architectural Enhancements}
We investigated several strategies to improve robustness and generalization:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Colorized Depth}: We augmented RGB observations with depth maps colorized using a JET gradient. This allows the model to perceive the 3D structure of the cloth, such as folds and wrinkles, while leveraging pre-trained visual features.
    \item \textbf{Data Augmentations}: We implemented random cropping, rotation, and color jittering to simulate variations in garment pose and sensor noise.
    \item \textbf{DINOv2 Backbone}: We compared the standard ResNet-18 visual backbone with DINOv2. The use of DINOv2 provided a richer feature representation, leading to improved success rates in complex garment categories.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Parameters}: We performed a systematic analysis of the chunk size and the number of execution steps (\texttt{n\_action\_steps}). We found that executing a single action step before re-predicting the chunk (\texttt{n\_action\_steps} = 1) provided the best performance by allowing the model to remain responsive to the current state.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation Constraints}
Our study was conducted on a shared HPC environment equipped with an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU. Due to high demand and the lack of a graphical interface, we were unable to implement online human-in-the-loop corrections. These constraints informed our focus on data efficiency and offline adaptation strategies.

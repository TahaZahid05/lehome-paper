\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis: Chunk Size and Action Steps}

We investigated the impact of the action chunk size and the number of execution steps in ACT. Our findings suggest that the \texttt{chunk\_size} is a critical hyperparameter for long-horizon bimanual tasks. 

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Small Chunks (< 30)}: The model exhibits disjointed movements and fails to capture the smooth coordination needed for the full folding sequence.
    \item \textbf{Medium Chunks (50-100)}: Provides the best balance, allowing the model to plan a coherent trajectory while remaining responsive to the current state.
    \item \textbf{Large Chunks (> 150)}: Leads to an accumulation of errors; if the initial prediction is slightly inaccurate, the robot drifts significantly from the desired path before the next chunk is predicted.
\end{itemize}

The number of \texttt{n\_action\_steps} (the execution window) also plays a role in trajectory smoothness. We found that execution steps slightly smaller than the chunk size (e.g., 50 steps for a chunk of 60) allow for temporal smoothing during the transition between chunks.

\subsection{Failure Analysis and Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Failure modes vary across models. DP often results in catastrophic entanglement, while VLA models tend to miss-identify the specific part of the cloth to grasp. ACT failures are typically "graceful," such as a missed grasp that leaves the garment in a stable state. The "Top Short" category remains the most difficult due to the minimal surface area for grasping the sleeves. Infrastructure constraints, such as the lack of a GUI for real-time human intervention, limited our ability to address these edge cases through online corrective feedback.
